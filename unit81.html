Unit 81: Using "Some," "Most," "All," "None," and "Any"

Home About Courses Contact

Introduction

Objective: By the end of this unit, learners will understand how to use "some," "most," "all," "none," and "any" correctly in various contexts.

Overview: This unit focuses on using these quantifiers to refer to different amounts or portions of something.

Explanation

Using "Some" and "Most": Use "some" to refer to an unspecified quantity that is less than the whole, and "most" to refer to the majority of something.

  • Example: Some people never stop talking. Most children like playing.

Using "All": Use "all" to refer to the entire group or total amount.

  • Example: All the students failed the exam.

Using "None": Use "none" to refer to the absence of something.

  • Example: None of my friends are married.

Using "Any": Use "any" in negative statements and questions to refer to an unspecified quantity.

  • Example: Do you know any of the people in this photograph?

Examples

Using "Some," "Most," "All," "None," and "Any":

  • I don’t like any of the pictures in the living room.
  • Some of the shops in the city centre close at 6:30.
  • Most birds can fly.
  • None of my friends are married.
  • Do you know any of the people in this photograph?

Practice Exercises

Exercise 81.1: Complete the sentences. Use the word in brackets (some/most etc.). Sometimes you need "of" (some of/most of etc.)

  • 1. Most children like playing. (most)
  • 2. Some of this money is yours. (some)
  • 3. Some people never stop talking. (some)
  • 4. Most of the shops in the city centre close at 6:30. (most)
  • 5. You can change your money in most banks. (most)
  • 6. I don’t like any of the pictures in the living room. (any)
  • 7. He's lost all or all of his money. (all)
  • 8. None of my friends are married.
  • 9. Do you know any of the people in this photograph? (any)
  • 10. Most birds can fly. (most)
  • 11. I enjoyed most of the film, but I didn’t like the ending. (most)
  • 12. Some sports are very dangerous. (some)
  • 13. We can't find anywhere to stay. All or all of the hotels are full. (all)
  • 14. You must have some of this cheese. It’s delicious. (some)
  • 15. The weather was bad when we were on holiday. It rained most of the time. (most)

Exercise 81.2: Look at the pictures and answer the questions. Use: all/most/some/none + of them / of it

  • 1. How many of the people are women? Most of them.
  • 2. How many of the boxes are on the table? All of them.
  • 3. How many of the men are wearing hats? Some of them.
  • 4. How many of the windows are open? None of them.
  • 5. How many of the people are standing? Most of them.
  • 6. How much of the money is Ben's? None of it.

Exercise 81.3: Are these sentences OK? Correct the sentences that are wrong.

  • 1. Most children like playing. (OK)
  • 2. All the students failed the exam. (OK)
  • 3. Some people work too hard. (Corrected: too instead of to)
  • 4. Some of the questions in the exam were very easy. (OK)
  • 5. I haven’t seen any of those people before. (OK)
  • 6. All insects have six legs. (OK)
  • 7. Have you read all these books? or Have you read all of these books? (OK, both forms are correct)
  • 8. Most of the students/Most students in our class are very nice. (OK, both forms are correct)
  • 9. Most of my friends are going to the party. (OK)
  • 10. I'm very tired this morning - I was awake most of the night. (OK)

Summary

In this unit, we learned how to use "some" to refer to an unspecified quantity that is less than the whole, "most" to refer to the majority of something, "all" to refer to the entire group or total amount, "none" to refer to the absence of something, and "any" in negative statements and questions to refer to an unspecified quantity. We practiced forming sentences using these structures to enhance communication skills.
