Unit 31: Using Must, Mustn't, Had to, and Don't Need to

Introduction

Objective: By the end of this unit, learners will understand how to use "must", "mustn't", "had to", and "don't need to" to express necessity, prohibition, obligation, and lack of necessity.

Overview: This unit focuses on using these modal verbs and phrases to talk about various requirements and permissions.

Explanation

Using "Must": Use "must" to express a strong necessity or requirement.

  • Example: I must finish my homework.

Using "Mustn't": Use "mustn't" to express prohibition or something that is not allowed.

  • Example: You mustn't talk during the exam.

Using "Had to": Use "had to" to express a necessity or obligation in the past.

  • Example: I had to call my teacher yesterday.

Using "Don't Need to": Use "don't need to" to express that something is not necessary.

  • Example: You don't need to bring anything to the party.

Examples

Necessity:

  • I must study for the test.
  • She must complete the assignment by tomorrow.

Prohibition:

  • You mustn't enter the restricted area.
  • They mustn't be late for the meeting.

Past Obligation:

  • He had to wake up early for work yesterday.
  • We had to finish the project by last Friday.

Lack of Necessity:

  • You don't need to worry about the deadline.
  • We don't need to book a table in advance.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 31.1: Complete the sentences. Use "must" + these verbs: be - eat - go - learn - meet - wash - win

  • 1. I'm feeling very hungry. I must eat something.
  • 2. Mark is a fascinating person. You must meet him.
  • 3. My hands are dirty. I must wash them.
  • 4. You must learn to cook. It will be a useful skill.
  • 5. I must go to the supermarket to buy some groceries.
  • 6. The match today is crucial for us. We must win.
  • 7. You can't always have everything you want instantly. You must be patient.

Exercise 31.2: Write "I must" or "I had to"

  • 1. I had to walk home last night as there were no buses.
  • 2. It's getting late. I must leave now.
  • 3. I don't usually work on Sundays, but last Sunday I had to work.
  • 4. I must get up early tomorrow to catch my flight.
  • 5. I traveled to Berlin by train last week. The train was full, so I had to stand the whole journey.
  • 6. I was nearly late for my appointment this morning. I had to run to get there on time.
  • 7. I forgot to call Alice yesterday. I must call her later today.

Exercise 31.3: Complete the sentences. Use "mustn't" or "don't need to" + these verbs: forget - go - lose - rush - wait

  • 1. I don't need to go home yet. I can stay a bit longer.
  • 2. We have plenty of time. We don't need to rush.
  • 3. Keep these documents safe. You mustn't lose them.
  • 4. I'm not ready yet, but you don't need to wait for me. You can leave now and I'll join you later.
  • 5. We mustn't forget to turn off the lights before we leave.

Exercise 31.4: Write must / mustn't / had to / don't need to

  • 1. You don't need to leave now. You can stay here if you want.
  • 2. It's an amazing movie. You must watch it.
  • 3. The restaurant won't be crowded tonight. We don't need to book a table.
  • 4. I was very busy last month. I had to work overtime.
  • 5. I want to know what happened. You must tell me everything.
  • 6. You mustn't reveal this information to anyone. It's confidential.
  • 7. I must hurry or I'll miss my train.
  • 8. 'Why were you late?' 'I had to wait for a taxi, which took forever.'
  • 9. It's Emma's birthday next week. I mustn't forget to buy her a gift.

Summary

In this unit, we learned how to use "must", "mustn't", "had to", and "don't need to" to express necessity, prohibition, obligation, and lack of necessity. We practiced forming sentences using these modal verbs and phrases to talk about various requirements and permissions in different contexts.
