Unit 46: Forming Questions with "Who" and "What," and Other Question Types

Introduction

Objective: By the end of this unit, learners will understand how to form questions with "who" and "what," as well as other question types involving prepositions, which, and asking for descriptions.

Overview: This unit focuses on using "who" and "what" to form questions that inquire about people and things, as well as other question structures.

Explanation

Questions with "Who" and "What" as the Subject: Use "who" and "what" to ask about the subject of a sentence.

  • Example: Somebody broke the window. Who broke the window?

Questions with "Who" and "What" as the Object: Use "who" and "what" to ask about the object of a sentence.

  • Example: I bought something. What did you buy?

Questions with Prepositions: Form questions where the preposition comes at the end.

  • Example: I went to the cinema with someone. Who did you go to the cinema with?

Questions with "Which": Use "which" to ask about a specific item or person from a known set.

  • Example: Tom's father is in a hospital. Which hospital is he in?

Questions Asking for Descriptions: Use "What is/are... like?" to ask for descriptions.

  • Example: What is the weather like?

Examples

Questions with "Who" and "What" as the Subject:

  • Somebody wants to see you. Who wants to see me?
  • Something made me ill. What made you ill?

Questions with "Who" and "What" as the Object:

  • I phoned somebody. Who did you phone?
  • Jane did something. What did Jane do?

Questions with Prepositions:

  • The letter is from someone. Who is the letter from?
  • I gave the money to someone. Who did you give the money to?

Questions with "Which":

  • Tom's father is in a hospital. Which hospital is he in?
  • Jack plays for a football team. Which (football) team does he play for?

Questions Asking for Descriptions:

  • I want to know about the roads. What are the roads like?
  • I want to know about the weather. What is the weather like?

Practice Exercises

Exercise 46.1: You want the missing information (XXXXX). Write questions with "who" or "what"

  • 1. The letter is from XXXXX. Who is the letter from?
  • 2. I'm looking for a XXXXX. What are you looking for?
  • 3. I went to the cinema with XXXXX. Who did you go to the cinema with?
  • 4. The film was about XXXXX. What/Who was the film about?
  • 5. I gave the money to XXXXX. Who did you give the money to?
  • 6. The book was written by XXXXX. Who was the book written by?

Exercise 46.2: Write questions about the people in the pictures. Use these verbs + a preposition:

  • go - listen - look - talk - talk - wait
  • 1. Who is she talking to?
  • 2. What are they looking at?
  • 3. Which restaurant is he going to?
  • 4. What are they talking about?
  • 5. What is she listening to?
  • 6. Which bus are they waiting for?

Exercise 46.3: Write questions beginning with "Which..."

  • 1. Tom's father is in hospital. Which hospital is he in?
  • 2. We stayed at a hotel. Which hotel did you stay at?
  • 3. Jack plays for a football team. Which (football) team does he play for?
  • 4. I went to school in this town. Which school did you go to?

Exercise 46.4: You want some information about another country. You ask somebody who has been there. Ask questions with "What is/are... like?"

  • 1. (the roads) What are the roads like?
  • 2. (the food) What is the food like?
  • 3. (the people) What are the people like?
  • 4. (the weather) What is the weather like?

Exercise 46.5: Ask questions with "What was/were... like?"

  • 1. Your friend has just come back from holiday. Ask about the weather. What was the weather like?
  • 2. Your friend has just come back from the cinema. Ask about the film. What was the film like?
  • 3. Your friend has just finished an English course. Ask about the lessons. What were the lessons like?
  • 4. Your friend has just come back from holiday. Ask about the hotel. What was the hotel like?

Summary

In this unit, we learned how to form questions with "who" and "what" to ask about the subject or object of a sentence. We practiced forming questions using these structures to enhance communication skills.
