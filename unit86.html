Introduction

Objective: By the end of this unit, learners will understand how to use adverbs correctly in various contexts.

Overview: This unit focuses on using adverbs to describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs, and the difference between adverbs and adjectives.

Explanation

Adverbs: Adverbs describe or modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They often end in "-ly."

  • Example: He runs quickly.

Adjectives vs. Adverbs: Adjectives describe nouns, while adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs.

  • Example: She is a careful driver. (adjective) She drives carefully. (adverb)

Examples

Using Adverbs:

  • It's raining heavily.
  • He sings very badly.
  • She can run very fast.

  • 2. He sings very badly.
  • 3. They came in quietly.
  • 4. She shouted at me angrily.
  • 5. She can run very fast.

Exercise 86.2: Complete the sentences. Choose from the boxes

  • Box-1: come - know - sleep - win - explain - think - work
  • Box-2: carefully - clearly - easily - hard - quickly - well
  • 1. I'm going to tell you something very important, so please listen carefully.
  • 2. They work hard. At the end of the day they're always tired.
  • 3. I'm tired this morning. I didn't sleep well last night.
  • 4. You play tennis much better than me. When we play, you always win easily.
  • 5. Think carefully before you answer the question.
  • 6. I've met Alice a few times, but I don't know her very well.
  • 7. Our teacher doesn't explain things very clearly/well. We never understand him.
  • 8. Helen! I need your help. Come quickly!

Exercise 86.3: Which is right?

  • 1. Don't eat so quickly. It's not good for you.
  • 2. Why are you angry? I haven't done anything.
  • 3. Can you speak slowly, please?
  • 4. Come on, Dave! Why are you always so slow?
  • 5. Bill is a very careful driver.
  • 6. Jane is studying hard for her examinations.
  • 7. 'Where's Diane?' 'She was here, but she left suddenly.'
  • 8. Please be quiet. I'm studying.
  • 9. Some companies pay their workers very badly.
  • 10. Those oranges look nice. Can I have one?
  • 11. I don't remember much about the accident. Everything happened quickly.

Exercise 86.4: Write "good" or "well"

  • 1. Your English is very good. You speak it very well.
  • 2. Jackie did very well in her exams.
  • 3. The party was very good. I enjoyed it very much.
  • 4. Martin has a difficult job, but he does it well.
  • 5. How are your parents? Are they well?
  • 6. Did you have a good holiday? Was the weather good?

Summary

In this unit, we learned how to use adverbs to describe or modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. We also learned the difference between adjectives and adverbs. Adjectives describe nouns, while adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. We practiced forming sentences using these structures to enhance communication skills.
