Unit 94: Adverbs of Frequency and Placement

Home About Courses Contact

Introduction

Objective: By the end of this unit, learners will understand how to use adverbs of frequency (often, never, always, etc.) and their placement in sentences correctly.

Overview: This unit focuses on using adverbs of frequency to describe how often an action occurs and understanding their correct placement in sentences.

Explanation

Adverbs of Frequency: Adverbs of frequency describe how often an action occurs. They include words like always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, and never.

  • Example: Paul often plays tennis.

Placement of Adverbs: Adverbs of frequency typically come before the main verb but after the verb "to be."

  • Example: He always gets up early. (before the main verb)
  • Example: He is always on time. (after the verb "to be")

Examples

Using Adverbs of Frequency:

  • Paul often plays tennis.
  • He always gets up early.
  • He is never late for work.

Using "Also":

  • Yes, and I also play tennis.
  • Yes, and I also speak French.

Using "Both" and "All":

  • They both live in London.
  • They are all married.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 94.1: Write sentences about Paul with often/never etc.

  • 1. Paul often plays tennis.
  • 2. He always gets up early.
  • 3. He is never late for work.
  • 4. He sometimes gets angry.
  • 5. He rarely goes swimming.
  • 6. He is usually at home in the evenings.

Exercise 94.2: Write these sentences with never/always/usually etc.

  • 1. My brother never speaks to me.
  • 2. Susan is always polite.
  • 3. I usually finish work at 5 o'clock.
  • 4. Sarah has just started a new job.
  • 5. I rarely go to bed before midnight.
  • 6. The bus isn't usually late.
  • 7. I don't often eat fish.
  • 8. I will never forget what you said.
  • 9. Have you ever lost your passport?
  • 10. Do you still work in the same place?
  • 11. They always stay in the same hotel.
  • 12. Jane doesn't usually work on Saturdays.
  • 13. Is Tina already here?
  • 14. What do you usually have for breakfast?
  • 15. I can never remember his name.

Exercise 94.3: Write sentences with also

  • 1. Do you play football? (tennis) → Yes, and I also play tennis.
  • 2. Do you speak Italian? (French) → Yes, and I also speak French.
  • 3. Are you tired? (hungry) → Yes, and I'm also hungry.
  • 4. Have you been to England? (Ireland) → Yes, and I've also been to Ireland.
  • 5. Did you buy any clothes? (some books) → Yes, and I also bought some books.

Exercise 94.4: Write sentences with both and all

  • Same information for 2 boys:
    • I live in London.
    • I play football.
    • I'm a student.
    • I've got a car.
    • Sentences:
      • 1. They both live in London.
      • 2. They both play football.
      • 3. They are both students.
      • 4. They've both got cars. / They both have cars.
  • Same information for 3 girls:
    • I'm married.
    • I was born in England.
    • I live in New York.
    • Sentences:
      • 1. They are all married.
      • 2. They were all born in England.
      • 3. They all live in New York.

Summary

In this unit, we learned how to use adverbs of frequency to describe how often an action occurs. We also learned the correct placement of adverbs of frequency in sentences, using "also" to add additional information, and using "both" and "all" to refer to two or more subjects. We practiced forming sentences using these structures to enhance communication skills.
